State estimation using LS

Overview

[Code]
  • $x$ is the state vector to be estimated. It is assumed to be constant when a measurement is made, meaning the vehicle is not moving
  • $H$ is a matrix mapping the state space to the measurement space
  • $y$ is the vector of $m$ noisy measurements

  • The measurement model is given as: $$ y = Hx + v $$

  • The noise distribution is assumed to be gaussian. $v$ is sampled from the gaussian $\mathcal{N}(0, R)$

Collecting measurements

In the implemented example, if $x$ is a 4-element state vector, then in the ideal case with no noise, a single measurement would be: $$\begin{align*} \tilde{y} &= Hx \\ \tilde{y} &= \begin{bmatrix}t^3 & t^2 & t & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix}x_1 \\ x_2 \\ x_3 \\ x_4 \end{bmatrix} \\ \tilde{y} &= x_1t^3 + x_2t^2 + x_3t + x_4 \end{align*}$$ $x$ is the unknown to be estimated. However, to generate the measurements, $x$ is randomly generated and mixed with noise to produce $\tilde{y}$: $$ \tilde{y} = Hx + v $$ For all measurments, $x$ remains the same. However, $t$ (sampled from a uniform distribution) and $v$ (sampled from a gaussian distribution) changes.

$\tilde{y}$ is a polynomial of order len(x) - 1, i.e. a 4-element state produces a 3rd order polynomial. In this case the state $x$ are the coefficients of the polynomial. By generating observations from evaluating the polynomial at several different points (different values of $t$) we can gain a good approximation of the original state. Recovering a good approximation of the state requires more samples as the order of the polynomial increases.

Estimating the state/parameter

Given enough samples, $\hat{x}$ should converge to $x$. The exact number of measurements required depends on:
  • The complexity of mapping the state space to the measurement space using $H$
  • How noisy the measurements are, determined by $v$
