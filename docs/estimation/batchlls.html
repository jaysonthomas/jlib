State estimation using Batch LS

Overview

[Code]
  • $x$ is the unknown state vector to be estimated of size $n$. It is assumed to be constant when a measurement is made, meaning the vehicle is not moving
  • $H$ is a matrix mapping the state space to the measurement space
  • $y$ is the vector of $m$ noisy measurements - the more the samples, the more accurate the estimate $\hat{x}$ will be

  • The measurement model is given as: $$ y = Hx + v $$

  • The noise distribution is assumed to be gaussian. $v$ is sampled from the gaussian $\mathcal{N}(0, R)$

Setup the state

$x$ is randomly generated or can be initialised with any arbitrary values. For all measurments, $x$ remains the same. However, $t$ (sampled from a uniform distribution) and $v$ (sampled from a gaussian distribution) changes.

Create the Jacobian


# Generate a vector of m random values picked between -5 and 5 
# with equal probability.
t = np.random.uniform(-5, 5, m)
$H$ is a matrix (m, n) of $m$ rows. Each row, representing a polynomial of order $n-1$ ($n==4$), is created using a randomly picked $t$ value from a uniform distribution: $$ H_i = \begin{bmatrix}t_i^3 & t_i^2 & t_i^1 & t_i^0 \end{bmatrix} $$

Collect measurements

An ideal single measurement is of the form: $$\begin{align*} y &= Hx \\ y &= \begin{bmatrix}t^3 & t^2 & t & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix}x_1 \\ x_2 \\ x_3 \\ x_4 \end{bmatrix} \\ y &= x_1t^3 + x_2t^2 + x_3t + x_4 \end{align*}$$ To generate the $m$ noisy measurements, $x$ is mixed with gaussian noise $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$ to produce $y$: $$ y = Hx + v $$ 

y = H @ x + np.random.normal(0, 1, size=(m))

In this case the state $x$ are the coefficients of the polynomial. By generating observations from evaluating the polynomial at several different points (different values of $t$) we can gain a good approximation of the original state. Recovering a good approximation of the state requires more samples as the order of the polynomial increases.

Estimate the state

Given enough samples, $\hat{x}$ should converge to $x$. The exact number of measurements required depends on:
  • The complexity of mapping the state space to the measurement space using $H$
  • How noisy the measurements are, determined by $v$

xHat = LA.pinv(H.T @ H) @ H.T @ y
